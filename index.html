Chapter 1 — How We Measured It

Here's how we measured
timing discipline
across the data.

Every outbound sequence has a built-in schedule — Step 1 on Day 0, follow-up on Day 3, persistence call on Day 7. We measured how closely SDRs stuck to that schedule for each prospect, then compared scores between those who converted and those who didn't. The chapters that follow show the results.

What we studied

Two AU outbound sequences — the Relevance AI sequence (Seq 868), using AI-generated personalised emails, and the EOS Generic sequence (Seq 343), retired January 2025 — then validated against two UK sequences.

What "cohesion" means

Cohesion = how closely an SDR worked a prospect to the intended schedule. We measure the gap between when a step was actually completed versus when it was supposed to be completed. Zero delay = perfect cadence.

Who we scored

Three groups per sequence: converters (became opportunities), active prospects (still in sequence), and finished non-converters (every touchpoint completed, no conversion). 238 prospects total.

Score Total journey drift vs. intended schedule What it means
100 Exited on or ahead of schedule Perfect timing
85 Total journey up to 1 day behind schedule Healthy — minor drift

50 Total journey 2–4 days behind schedule At-Risk — falling behind
30 Total journey 4–7 days behind schedule Critical — significantly behind
10 Total journey more than 7 days behind schedule Critical — prospect deprioritised

Note on how the score works: This is a single end-to-end measurement, not a per-step average. It compares the prospect's total time in the sequence against what the schedule said that journey should have taken. A score of 85 means the full journey finished roughly 1 day later than intended — it doesn't tell you which step slipped. However, cumulative drift compounds: a prospect who reaches Step 10 only 1 day late must have been broadly on schedule throughout. You cannot rack up significant delays across multiple steps and still score well end-to-end. Cohesion also measures timing only, not quality — a score of 100 means the SDR stayed on cadence, not that the call was good or the email was compelling.

A note on our current overdue reporting: The company-wide weekly overdue process focuses primarily on prospects 7+ days behind their due date — which maps to Score 10 (Critical) in this framework. The data in the following chapters suggests that threshold may be too permissive. A prospect worked just 2–4 days late (Score 50) is already estimated to be one-third as likely to convert as a prospect worked on time. The meaningful conversion impact begins well before the 7-day mark — which means our current tolerance for "light" overdue may be more costly than it looks.

Chapter 3 — The Main Objection
"Wait. Hot prospects reply early. Early replies end the sequence quickly. High cohesion might just mean you got a quick convert — not that the SDR did anything special."

The most important objection to this analysis — and a legitimate one.

If responsive, high-quality prospects are the ones who convert — and they reply quickly, finishing the sequence early — then we're not measuring SDR discipline at all. We're measuring prospect quality. The SDR didn't earn that cohesion score; the prospect handed it to them by being easy to convert.

To test this, we needed a group of prospects who were fully worked through the entire sequence but still didn't convert. If high cohesion is just an artefact of hot prospects, those fully-worked non-converters should score just as well. If SDR prioritisation is driving the pattern, they should score dramatically lower.

The mid-sequence band is the signal that matters most.

The early-exit structural advantage is real — we're not hiding it. A prospect who books on the first email will score 100 almost automatically. That explains part of the gap in the early band.

But 59% of AU converters — the majority of the cohort — converted at Steps 5–8 (Days 1–5). No early-exit advantage applies here. These prospects took days to respond. Their average cohesion (60.6) still exceeds finished non-converters (45.2) by +15.4 points. That's the most conservative estimate of the cohesion signal, and it still holds.

In the UK, the mid-sequence gap is even larger: the 11% of converters in Steps 5–8 average 85.0 versus 35.2 — a +49.8 gap — with no structural advantage in play. The UK sample is small, but the direction is unambiguous.

The late-sequence band (Steps 9+) rounds out the picture: these converters scored 10.0, below the non-converter baseline. Low cohesion in late steps appears to suppress, not enable, conversion. The pattern across all three bands is consistent: cadence quality predicts outcomes in the middle of the sequence, where it's entirely within the SDR's control.

Chapter 8 — The Full Picture

The signal is real.
Here's what it means
and what to do with it.

The conversations about MQL thresholds, ICP fit, enrichment quality, and SDR tenure are all legitimate — and each of them requires time, investment, or structural change to move. This report is about what we can change right now, without spending anything extra. The data points to a single, controllable behaviour as the most consistent predictor of conversion in our outbound motion: working sequence tasks on schedule.

Yes.

Across every cohort we measured — AU and UK, AI-personalised and fixed template — prospects who converted were worked more consistently to schedule than prospects who didn't. The gap persists after controlling for prospect quality and SDR talent. It is the most consistent signal in this dataset. And crucially, it is one we can act on immediately — the timing of these touchpoints is entirely within our control.

The ~6× means a perfectly-worked prospect shows up in the "on time" band roughly six times more often (proportionally) than a critically-delayed one.

Here is what that means precisely — and where the evidence runs out.

What the data supports

  • In the AU Rel AI sequence, converters were consistently worked closer to schedule than non-converters — a 19-point average gap (64.3 vs 45.2).
  • Converters were twice as likely to be worked within 4 days of schedule: 76% of converters vs 50% of non-converters.
  • The gap survived two controls: prospect quality (fully-worked non-converters) and SDR talent (9 of 11 reps showed the pattern within their own books of work).
  • The same direction holds across scale. When we looked at every outbound opportunity created in May 2026 with sequence attribution — 77 converter prospects across 11 sequences — 9 of 11 showed the same pattern, with an average gap of +20 points. The signal doesn't disappear when the sample gets broader.
  • Cohesion scoring is a viable leading indicator for SDR prioritisation quality.

What the data doesn't prove

  • Causality. We can't definitively separate "good cadence causes conversion" from "engaged prospects inspire good cadence." Both are likely true to some degree.
  • Full SDR control. The rep-by-rep test helps rule out the "better reps just work faster" argument — but what remains is the possibility that SDRs instinctively work harder on prospects showing early interest, meaning the prospect is still partly driving the score.
  • Early exit bias is real. 39% of AU converters finished at Step 1–4 (Day 0). When excluded, the gap narrows from 19 to 13 points — but does not disappear.
  • UK results are directional only. n=9 for UK Rel AI and n=4 for UK New HR Leader are too small for confident conclusions. UK New HR Leader is a hypothesis, not a finding.
  • This is observational data. A controlled experiment is required to prove causality definitively.
The Opportunity

No new tools. No enrichment budget. No structural changes required.

Improving ICP quality requires data investment. Adjusting MQL criteria requires business-wide alignment. Changing the tenure mix of the SDR team takes time and hiring cycles. Improving cadence discipline requires none of those things. The timing of sequence touchpoints is a behaviour — and behaviour is something we can change today. This data tells us that heightened diligence in working tasks on schedule is the most direct lever we currently have on our MQL to SAO conversion rate, and it costs nothing to act on beyond the commitment to hold it as a standard.

There is one nuance worth naming: our weekly overdue reporting currently focuses on prospects 7+ days past due. That creates a risk of tolerating delays that this data says already matter. A prospect slipping 2–4 days behind schedule is already estimated at one-third the conversion likelihood of a prospect worked on time. The goal is zero tolerance for any overdue — not just the heavy ones. Supporting this will likely require a look at sequence design: higher automation of touch steps, and where appropriate, shorter overall sequences that reduce the task volume SDRs carry and give them the capacity to hold tighter to schedule on every prospect they work.

Three actions this data supports
01
Raise the standard — zero tolerance for any overdue, not just 7-day
Our current weekly overdue reporting flags prospects 7+ days past due. The data suggests this creates tolerance for delays that already hurt: a prospect 2–4 days late is estimated to be a third as likely to convert. The standard should shift to flagging any prospect behind schedule, not just the critically overdue ones. Enabling this long-term may mean reviewing sequence design — more automation of touch steps and tighter sequence lengths — to reduce the task volume SDRs need to manage and make zero-overdue a realistic expectation, not just an aspiration.
02
Grow the UK converter sample before drawing conclusions
UK Rel AI has 9 converters — too few to act on. The 22-point gap is directionally interesting but not yet reliable. Re-analyse when n ≥ 25. Also check whether UK SDRs understand the sequence cadence expectations as clearly as AU SDRs do.
03
Run a controlled test to prove causality
To answer the causal question definitively: randomly assign prospects to a "cadence-enforced" group (daily check-in on sequence delays) versus standard working. Compare conversion rates after 60 days. This is the only way to move from correlation to proof.

Methodology note

Cohesion score = single end-to-end measurement, not a per-step average. Delay = prospect journey days (stateChangedAt − createdAt) minus the cumulative scheduled day for the final step completed. This produces one number per prospect reflecting how far the total journey drifted from the intended schedule. Step schedules derived from Outreach API interval fields. Cohesion proxy does not distinguish call completion from email automation — it measures total elapsed time against intended schedule. Note: individual step timing is not captured; however, because drift compounds across steps, a prospect who exits with low overall delay must have been broadly on schedule throughout their journey.

Converter cohorts sourced from Salesforce opportunity exports. Finished non-converter cohorts are random samples (seed=42, n=50) from Outreach API sequence states with state="finished". Active pool for AU Rel AI is the full prospect set at time of analysis.

Scale test (The Scale Test chapter): 87 prospects from a May 2026 opportunity export across 11 outbound sequences. Cohesion scores computed per-sequence using that sequence's step schedule. Non-converter benchmark = 10 most-recently-finished non-converter states per sequence. Two sequences excluded from headline averages due to data quality (sampling artefact and n<3).
