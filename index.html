The Problem

Current monitoring sees
one step at a time.
Here's what it misses.

Outreach flags a task the moment it's overdue. That's the system working as designed. But it only ever looks at right now. The eleven steps that came before — whether they were on time, two days late, or a week late — are invisible. Two prospects can show an identical status in your dashboard today while having completely different histories.

Chapter 1 — How We Measured It

Here's how we measured
timing discipline
across the data.

Every outbound sequence has a built-in schedule — Step 1 on Day 0, follow-up on Day 3, persistence call on Day 7. We measured how closely SDRs stuck to that schedule for each prospect, then compared scores between those who converted and those who didn't. The chapters that follow show the results.

What we studied

Two AU outbound sequences — the Relevance AI sequence (Seq 868), using AI-generated personalised emails, and the EOS Generic sequence (Seq 343), retired January 2025 — then validated against two UK sequences.

What "cohesion" means

Cohesion = how closely an SDR worked a prospect to the intended schedule. We measure the gap between when a step was actually completed versus when it was supposed to be completed. Zero delay = perfect cadence.

Who we scored

Three groups per sequence: converters (became opportunities), active prospects (still in sequence), and finished non-converters (every touchpoint completed, no conversion). 238 prospects total.

Note on scope: Cohesion measures timing, not quality. A score of 100 means a step was completed on schedule — it doesn't tell us whether the call was good or the email was compelling. Think of it as the scheduling discipline measure, not the effort measure.

Chapter 3 — The Main Objection
"Wait. Hot prospects reply early. Early replies end the sequence quickly. High cohesion might just mean you got a quick convert — not that the SDR did anything special."

The most important objection to this analysis — and a legitimate one.

If responsive, high-quality prospects are the ones who convert — and they reply quickly, finishing the sequence early — then we're not measuring SDR discipline at all. We're measuring prospect quality. The SDR didn't earn that cohesion score; the prospect handed it to them by being easy to convert.

To test this, we needed a group of prospects who were fully worked through the entire sequence but still didn't convert. If high cohesion is just an artefact of hot prospects, those fully-worked non-converters should score just as well. If SDR prioritisation is driving the pattern, they should score dramatically lower.

Why this addresses the hot prospect objection: The objection says high cohesion just means a prospect converted quickly, not that the SDR worked them well. But these 50 prospects went through the entire sequence. They weren't quick converts. They were worked all the way through — and they still didn't convert. If the SDR's cadence discipline didn't matter, these prospects should score similarly to converters. Instead, half were 4+ days behind schedule at every step. The same SDRs, working the same sequence, prioritised their prospects very differently.
The finding: Controlling for prospect quality doesn't make the gap disappear — it confirms it. Fully-worked non-converters received every touchpoint the converters did. They were the same ICP, on the same sequence, worked by the same team. The only measurable difference is how consistently the SDR kept to the schedule. That difference correlates with conversion.

Why the data doesn't fully support the objection, either.

The AU data is the most important here: it has the largest converter sample (n=41) and the lowest early-exit rate (39%). When we exclude the 16 Day-0 exits and look only at mid and late-sequence converters, the remaining 25 prospects average 58.6 — still above the 45.2 baseline for finished non-converters. The gap narrows from 19.1 to 13.4 points, but it does not disappear.

In UK, the early-exit rate is higher (67%), which makes this built-in scoring advantage more significant. UK Rel AI converters averaged 57.2 versus 35.2 — a 22-point gap — but with only 9 total converters, this points in the right direction without being strong enough to act on confidently yet.

This supports a nuanced read: cohesion may be acting as a proxy for SDR prioritisation quality rather than a direct cause of conversion. When an SDR invests attention in a prospect — maintaining cadence — the cohesion score captures that pattern. The early-exit advantage is real, but it does not account for the full gap.

The ~6× means a perfectly-worked prospect shows up in the "on time" band roughly six times more often (proportionally) than a critically-delayed one.

Here is what that means precisely — and where the evidence runs out.

What the data supports

  • In the AU Rel AI sequence, converters were consistently worked closer to schedule than non-converters — a 19-point average gap (64.3 vs 45.2).
  • Converters were twice as likely to be worked within 4 days of schedule: 76% of converters vs 50% of non-converters.
  • The gap survived two controls: prospect quality (fully-worked non-converters) and SDR talent (9 of 11 reps showed the pattern within their own books of work).
  • The same direction holds across all three sequences tested: AU Rel AI, UK Rel AI, and UK New HR Leader.
  • Cohesion scoring is a viable leading indicator for SDR prioritisation quality.

What the data doesn't prove

  • Causality. We can't definitively separate "good cadence causes conversion" from "engaged prospects inspire good cadence." Both are likely true to some degree.
  • Full SDR control. The rep-by-rep test helps rule out the "better reps just work faster" argument — but what remains is the possibility that SDRs instinctively work harder on prospects showing early interest, meaning the prospect is still partly driving the score.
  • Early exit bias is real. 39% of AU converters finished at Step 1–4 (Day 0). When excluded, the gap narrows from 19 to 13 points — but does not disappear.
  • UK results are directional only. n=9 for UK Rel AI and n=4 for UK New HR Leader are too small for confident conclusions. UK New HR Leader is a hypothesis, not a finding.
  • This is observational data. A controlled experiment is required to prove causality definitively.
Three actions this data supports
01
Start tracking cadence as a weekly SDR metric
Flag any prospect where the SDR is 4+ days behind schedule for manager review. This is actionable regardless of whether cadence causes conversion or just signals prioritisation — either way, a 4+ day delay is a problem worth catching.
02
Grow the UK converter sample before drawing conclusions
UK Rel AI has 9 converters — too few to act on. The 22-point gap is directionally interesting but not yet reliable. Re-analyse when n ≥ 25. Also check whether UK SDRs understand the sequence cadence expectations as clearly as AU SDRs do.
03
Run a controlled test to prove causality
To answer the causal question definitively: randomly assign prospects to a "cadence-enforced" group (daily check-in on sequence delays) versus standard working. Compare conversion rates after 60 days. This is the only way to move from correlation to proof.

Methodology note

Cohesion score = proxy measurement based on sequence state timing. Delay = prospect journey days (stateChangedAt − createdAt) minus the cumulative scheduled day for the final step completed. Step schedules derived from Outreach API interval fields. Cohesion proxy does not distinguish call completion from email automation — it measures total elapsed time against intended schedule.

Converter cohorts sourced from Salesforce opportunity exports. Finished non-converter cohorts are random samples (seed=42, n=50) from Outreach API sequence states with state="finished". Active pool for AU Rel AI is the full prospect set at time of analysis.
