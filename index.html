Chapter 1 — The Hypothesis

Does working a sequence
to cadence
actually predict conversion?

Every outbound sequence has a schedule. Step 1 on Day 0. Follow-up on Day 3. Persistence call on Day 7. The cadence exists for a reason: consistent, timely contact maximises the chance a prospect is available and ready to engage. But does the data support this? Do SDRs who work their sequences on schedule actually convert more?

📋

What we studied

Two AU outbound sequences — the Relevance AI sequence (Seq 868), which uses AI-generated personalised emails, and the EOS Generic sequence (Seq 343), retired January 2025. Then validated against two UK sequences.

📐

What is cohesion?

Cohesion measures how closely the timing of a prospect's sequence completion matched the intended schedule. Delay = actual journey days minus scheduled step day. Zero or negative delay means the SDR was on cadence or ahead.

👥

Who we scored

Three cohorts per sequence: confirmed converters (prospects who became opportunities), the active prospect pool, and finished non-converters (fully worked, no conversion). Total: 238 prospects across all cohorts.

Important scope note: Cohesion measures the timing of sequence execution, not the quality of individual touchpoints. A score of 100 means a step was completed on schedule — it says nothing about whether the call was good or the email was compelling. High cohesion is a necessary but not sufficient condition for effective outreach.

Chapter 3 — The Alternative Explanation
"Wait. Hot prospects reply early. Early replies end the sequence quickly. Short sequences score 100 by default."

The most important objection to this analysis — and a legitimate one.

If responsive, high-quality prospects are the ones who convert — and they also reply on Day 0, finishing the sequence early with high cohesion — then we're not measuring SDR discipline at all. We're measuring prospect quality. The high cohesion is a byproduct of the prospect being "hot," not evidence that the SDR's cadence had anything to do with it.

We needed a way to test this. We needed prospects who were fully worked through the sequence but didn't convert. If cadence is merely an artefact of prospect quality, their cohesion scores should look like whatever the SDR typically delivers — not dramatically lower.

Why this rules out the hot prospect theory: If responsive prospects drive both high cohesion and conversion, then fully-worked non-converters — given the same sequence by the same SDR team — should reflect the SDR's normal working cadence. Instead, 50% scored Critical (delay of 4+ days). The SDR clearly behaved differently with these prospects. They were deprioritised, and they didn't convert.
The finding: Cohesion tracks conversion — even when prospect quality is held constant. Fully-worked non-converters received every touchpoint. They were the same ICP, on the same sequence, run by the same team. The only meaningful difference is how consistently the SDR worked them. That difference is captured in the cohesion score, and it correlates with outcomes.

Cadence discipline — applied at the individual prospect level — tracks outcomes independently.

Why the data doesn't fully support the objection, either.

The AU data is the most important here: it has the largest converter sample (n=41) and the lowest early-exit rate (39%). When we exclude the 16 Day-0 exits and look only at mid and late-sequence converters, the remaining 25 prospects average 58.6 — still above the 45.2 baseline for finished non-converters. The gap narrows from 19.1 to 13.4 points, but it does not disappear.

In UK, the early-exit rate is higher (67%), making the structural bias more significant. UK Rel AI converters averaged 57.2 versus 35.2 — a 22-point gap — but with only 9 total converters, this remains directional rather than definitive.

This supports a nuanced interpretation: cohesion may function as a proxy for SDR prioritisation quality rather than a direct cause of conversion. When an SDR invests attention in a prospect — maintaining cadence — the cohesion score captures that pattern. The early-exit structural bias is real, but it does not account for the full gap.

Chapter 8 — The Honest Verdict

What the data says.
What it doesn't.

What we can claim

  • In the AU Rel AI sequence, there is a strong, consistent association between high cohesion scores and conversion to opportunity.
  • The 19-point average gap between converters (64.3) and finished non-converters (45.2) is consistent and meaningful. Converters are half as likely to fall into the Critical zone (24% vs 50%).
  • The finished cohort methodology rules out the simplest form of the hot prospect objection.
  • The direction of the signal holds across all three sequences tested: AU Rel AI, UK Rel AI, UK New HR Leader.
  • Cohesion scoring is a viable leading indicator for SDR prioritisation quality in AI-personalised sequences.

What we cannot claim

  • We cannot definitively separate "SDR cadence causes conversion" from "engaged prospects inspire SDR cadence." Both can be true simultaneously.
  • SDR quality is an unmeasured confound. Top SDRs likely maintain better cadence AND convert at higher rates — because they're better SDRs. Without controlling at the rep level, we cannot rule out that the signal measures who worked the prospect, not how they worked it.
  • Early-exit bias exists but does not explain the gap. AU converters have a 39% Day-0 exit rate (lower than UK's 67%). Excluding them, the remaining 25 mid/late-sequence converters still average 58.6 vs 45.2 — the gap narrows to 13.4 points but persists.
  • UK sample sizes are too small (n=9 and n=4) for cross-region claims. UK New HR Leader in particular cannot support any finding.
  • Causality requires a controlled experiment — this is observational data and should be treated as hypothesis-generating.
Three actions this data supports
01
Track cohesion as a weekly SDR metric
Implement cohesion scoring for AU Rel AI as a regular team visibility metric. Flag prospects with delays of 4+ days for manager review. This addresses the SDR prioritisation hypothesis regardless of which causal direction is correct.
02
Investigate the UK cohesion gap
UK Rel AI launched March 2025 and has a 9-converter sample. Before drawing conclusions about regional differences, expand the converter cohort to 30+ and re-analyse. Also audit whether UK SDRs understand the sequence cadence expectations.
03
Design a controlled test for causality
To answer the causal question definitively, randomly assign a cohort of prospects to a "high-cadence enforced" condition (daily manager check-in on sequence delays) versus standard working. Compare conversion rates after 60 days.

Methodology note

Cohesion score = proxy measurement based on sequence state timing. Delay = prospect journey days (stateChangedAt − createdAt) minus the cumulative scheduled day for the final step completed. Step schedules derived from Outreach API interval fields. Cohesion proxy does not distinguish call completion from email automation — it measures total elapsed time against intended schedule.

Converter cohorts sourced from Salesforce opportunity exports. Finished non-converter cohorts are random samples (seed=42, n=50) from Outreach API sequence states with state="finished". Active pool for AU Rel AI is the full prospect set at time of analysis.
