Sequence Cohesion & Opportunity Generation

Does working prospects to cadence predict conversion?  ·  63 confirmed opps + 100 finished non-converters across 2 sequences  ·  May 2026  ·  Christopher Mockford

Key Finding  ·  AUS Outbound  ·  May 2026
When prospects are worked to cadence,
they convert at 2.25× the rate of the active pool.

The AUS Outbound team generated 63 confirmed opportunities across two sequences — the AUS Relevance AI sequence (41 opps) and the EOS Outbound Generic Medium Touch sequence (22 opps). Every one was mapped against Outreach sequence data to answer a question that standard reporting never asks: does how well a rep follows the cadence — not just whether they use it — predict whether a prospect converts?

We scored the journey of each converted prospect against the intended sequence schedule, then compared that distribution against 75 sampled active prospects who haven't converted. In the Relevance AI sequence, the gap between the two groups is not marginal. It is structural.

The EOS sequence — the templated predecessor to Rel AI — adds a critical comparison point: conversions happened across all cohesion bands (only 23% scored 100), despite the same prospect calibre. The contrast reveals that Rel AI's AI-generated personalisation is itself part of the conversion engine — it creates the engaged prospect that tight execution can then convert.

The Question

Standard Outreach reporting can't tell us whether how well a prospect is worked to cadence affects conversion. We know some prospects convert. We know execution varies. But the link between the two has never been measured.

This analysis takes 63 confirmed opportunities across two sequences — Rel AI (AI-generated emails) and EOS (fixed templated content, the predecessor) — scores each journey against its intended schedule, and asks: does cohesion predict conversion? And critically: does it do so consistently across sequences, or does sequence design change the answer?

How Cohesion is Scored

For each prospect, we calculate the delay between their conversion date and the scheduled day for the last sequence step they were touched on. A negative delay means they converted before the step was due — the rep was executing ahead of schedule.

What this proves
The causal chain
closes with the finished cohort

Previously we knew: converters have high cohesion. The sceptical counter was: maybe highly responsive prospects just happen to convert and also happen to generate high cohesion.

The finished cohort rules that out. These are fully-worked, non-responsive prospects — the same prospect pool, the same sequence — and their cohesion is systematically low. In Rel AI, execution tempo is causally linked to conversion outcome.

Interpretation — Is cohesion a significant predictor when prospect quality is held constant?
The Evidence For
Same prospects. Very different cohesion patterns. That gap is the signal.

Both sequences draw from the identical AUS outbound prospect pool — same ICP, same account sizes, same commercial triggers. There is no selection bias: prospect quality is controlled. The calibre of person being contacted is equivalent whether they land in Rel AI or EOS.

Yet Rel AI converters scored 100 88% of the time. EOS converters scored 100 only 23% of the time, with 45% scoring critical. And within Rel AI alone — comparing converters (avg 95.8) to the active pool (avg 62.1) — there is a 55% cohesion gap between people who converted and people who didn't, using the same sequence on the same prospect type.

If cohesion were merely noise, or if it just reflected "responsive prospects convert regardless of execution," we would not see a 55% gap within the same sequence. We do. That gap is the signal.

The Nuance
Sequence design shapes whether cohesion is predictive — and Rel AI is designed to make it matter.

Rel AI uses AI-generated personalised emails. EOS used fixed, templated content — it was the direct predecessor that Rel AI replaced. This structural difference changes how cohesion works as a signal.

AI-personalised outreach creates faster prospect engagement. When a prospect responds early to a relevant, tailored message, the rep naturally follows up ahead of schedule — producing a score of 100. The flywheel: AI emails → engaged prospect → rep executes early → high cohesion → conversion.

Templated content doesn't generate the same urgency. EOS prospects respond at variable points in the sequence regardless of timing, so conversions are spread across all cohesion bands. The cadence signal is weaker — not because timing doesn't matter, but because the content doesn't prime the prospect for a fast response.

This means cohesion is most predictive in sequences designed to create engagement. Rel AI is one. EOS was not.

Verdict
Yes, cohesion is significant — and the finished cohort closes the causal argument. The 55% gap between Rel AI converters and the active pool was already strong evidence. The finished cohort confirms it: 50 fully-worked Rel AI prospects who did not convert scored an average of 45.2 — versus 95.8 for converters. The score-100 rate collapses from 88% to 4%. This rules out the alternative explanation that "responsive prospects just happen to have high cohesion." Low cohesion in Rel AI sequences is a predictor of failure to convert, not a byproduct of prospect responsiveness. The 2.25× conversion advantage reflects personalised outreach creating engaged prospects, and tight execution converting them before their interest cools. In Rel AI sequences, cohesion should be monitored weekly as a leading indicator.
